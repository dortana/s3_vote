% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Secure electronic voting demands confidentiality of individual ballots,
integrity of the final tally, public verifiability of correct execution,
and resilience against colluding or misbehaving participants — all without
relying on a single trusted authority.
This thesis presents \textbf{S$^3$-Vote} (\textit{Secure, Scalable,
Sovereign Voting}), a decentralised voting protocol that integrates
ElGamal threshold encryption, Shamir Secret Sharing, and
Publicly Verifiable Secret Sharing (PVSS) with a novel
\emph{reputation-weighted adaptive reconstruction threshold}.

Each decryption authority accumulates a persistent reputation score
derived from verifiable on-chain behaviour across elections, covering
participation, contribution, and honesty.
Reputation scores are converted into per-authority weights, and the
effective quorum adapts via a two-piece linear formula anchored at
$\lceil N/2 \rceil$ for neutral trust.
A \emph{two-gate reconstruction policy} simultaneously enforces a
cryptographic share-count gate and a reputation weight gate, closing
flooding and monopoly attacks that single-threshold schemes cannot prevent.
A concentration-resistance property is proven: no single authority can
accumulate sufficient weight to reconstruct alone.

Ballot privacy is formally proven as an IND-CPA security game with a
concrete reduction to the Decisional Diffie-Hellman (DDH) assumption
under the random oracle model.
A game-theoretic analysis shows that honest participation is the dominant
strategy for rational authorities, and that coalitions are inherently
unstable.
A parameter sensitivity analysis characterises the effect of each
reputation weight on system behaviour and bounds the consequences of
miscalibration.

All events are recorded on a hash-chained blockchain, providing a
tamper-evident, non-repudiable audit trail.
The system is compared against Shamir's threshold scheme, Nojoumian's
social secret sharing, and deployed e-voting systems (FOO92, Helios,
Belenios, Civitas) — none of which address adaptive thresholds, authority
accountability, or graceful failure recovery simultaneously.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

The integrity of voting systems is foundational to democratic governance and
organisational decision-making. Traditional electronic voting systems route
all trust through a central authority: a government agency, corporate IT
infrastructure, or a cloud provider.
This design introduces two compounding vulnerabilities simultaneously.

First, a \textbf{single point of trust}: the operator has complete power
to manipulate ballots, suppress results, or alter tallies, often without
any publicly verifiable evidence of wrongdoing. Second, a
\textbf{single point of failure}: if the operator's infrastructure is
compromised — through malicious attack, system failure, or coercion —
the entire election is ruined.

Distributed threshold cryptography was developed to address these concerns
by splitting trust across multiple independent parties. Yet classical
threshold schemes such as Shamir's foundational work~\cite{shamir1979}
treat every participant as equally trustworthy and provide no mechanism for
detecting, proving, or adapting to misbehaviour. A single malicious
authority submitting a corrupted share causes silent reconstruction failure,
with no accountability and no path to recovery.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The core problem this thesis addresses is:

\begin{insight}[Central Question]
\textit{How can we design a decentralised voting system where the
reconstruction threshold adapts dynamically to observed authority behaviour,
cheating is publicly provable rather than merely suspected, and the
system recovers gracefully from partial failures — all without relying
on any trusted coordinator?}
\end{insight}

\subsection{Contributions}

This thesis makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}, leftmargin=3em]
  \item \textbf{Reputation-weighted secret sharing.} A persistent,
    cross-election reputation model for decryption authorities based on
    three objectively measurable behavioural dimensions.

  \item \textbf{Two-piece linear adaptive threshold.} A provably anchored
    quorum formula that maps collective trust to a reconstruction requirement
    between $\lceil N/3 \rceil$ and $N-1$, initialising at exactly
    $\lceil N/2 \rceil$ for fresh authorities.

  \item \textbf{Two-gate reconstruction policy.} Simultaneous enforcement
    of a cryptographic share-count gate and a reputation weight gate,
    closing attack vectors that single-threshold schemes cannot prevent.

  \item \textbf{Concentration-resistance proof.} Formal demonstration that
    no single authority can ever accumulate sufficient weight to satisfy
    the quorum alone, regardless of their reputation.

  \item \textbf{End-to-end prototype.} A fully operational implementation
    comprising ElGamal encryption, Shamir sharing, PVSS verification,
    reputation engine, adaptive threshold, and blockchain audit log,
    accessible through a real-time web dashboard.

  \item \textbf{Formal ballot privacy proof.} A concrete IND-CPA security
    reduction from the S$^3$-Vote threshold ElGamal scheme to the DDH
    problem, with PVSS binding established under the discrete logarithm
    assumption and random oracle model.

  \item \textbf{Game-theoretic honesty dominance.} A formal proof that
    honest participation strictly dominates cheating for rational
    authorities over any sufficiently long horizon, and that colluding
    coalitions are inherently unstable under the reputation penalty
    mechanism.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{ElGamal Encryption}

ElGamal encryption~\cite{elgamal1985} is a public-key cryptosystem based on
the discrete logarithm problem, operating over a cyclic group
$\mathbb{Z}_p^*$ with a large prime $p$ and generator $g$.

\begin{definition}[ElGamal Key Pair]
  A key pair $(y, x)$ is generated by choosing a random private key
  $x \in \mathbb{Z}_{p-1}$ and computing public key $y = g^x \bmod p$.
\end{definition}

To encrypt a vote encoded as a group element $m \in \mathbb{Z}_p^*$:
\begin{align}
  C_1 &= g^k \bmod p \label{eq:eg1}\\
  C_2 &= m \cdot y^k \bmod p \label{eq:eg2}
\end{align}
where $k$ is a fresh random ephemeral value for each encryption.
Decryption recovers $m = C_2 \cdot (C_1^x)^{-1} \bmod p$.
The scheme is semantically secure under the Decisional Diffie-Hellman (DDH)
assumption~\cite{elgamal1985,boneh1998}.

In S$^3$-Vote, the private key $x$ is never held whole by any single party;
it is split using Shamir Secret Sharing before the election begins and
reconstructed only after all ballots are cast.

\subsection{Shamir Secret Sharing}

\begin{definition}[$(t,n)$-Threshold Scheme~\cite{shamir1979}]
  A secret $s \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ is split by constructing a random polynomial
  of degree $t{-}1$ with $f(0) = s$:
  \begin{equation}
    f(z) = s + a_1 z + a_2 z^2 + \cdots + a_{t-1} z^{t-1} \pmod{p}
  \end{equation}
  Authority $i$ receives share $s_i = f(i)$.
  Any $t$ shares uniquely determine $f$ via Lagrange interpolation:
  \begin{equation}
    s = f(0) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} s_i \prod_{j \in \mathcal{S},\, j \neq i}
    \frac{-j}{i-j} \pmod{p}
  \end{equation}
  Any $t-1$ shares reveal no information about $s$ (information-theoretic
  secrecy).
\end{definition}

The classical scheme treats all shares as interchangeable: any $t$-subset
of the $n$ shares suffices for reconstruction.
Desmedt and Frankel~\cite{desmedt1990} extended this idea to threshold
cryptosystems, enabling distributed decryption without reconstructing the
private key in full — a precursor to the threshold ElGamal decryption used
in S$^3$-Vote.
S$^3$-Vote further augments this with reputation weights, so that the
effective contribution of each authority to the reconstruction quorum depends
on their trust level.

\subsection{Publicly Verifiable Secret Sharing}

In standard secret sharing, a participant who submits a corrupted share
causes reconstruction failure with no way to publicly identify the culprit.
PVSS~\cite{schoenmakers1999,pedersen1991} solves this via cryptographic
\emph{commitments} that allow anyone to verify share correctness without
learning the secret.

\begin{definition}[PVSS Commitment]
  For share $s_i$ and cryptographic hash $H$, the commitment is:
  \begin{equation}
    C_i = g^{H(s_i)} \bmod p
    \label{eq:commitment}
  \end{equation}
  Commitments are published to the blockchain at election creation.
  During reconstruction, authority $i$ submits $\hat{s}_i$; the verifier
  checks $g^{H(\hat{s}_i)} \equiv C_i \pmod{p}$.
  A mismatch constitutes publicly verifiable proof of an invalid submission.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Blockchain Technology}

A blockchain is an append-only, distributed, hash-chained ledger.
Each block $B_k$ contains a data payload and a hash computed over both
the payload and the previous block's hash:
\begin{equation}
  h_k = \text{SHA-256}(\,k \,\|\, \text{timestamp}_k \,\|\,
        \text{data}_k \,\|\, h_{k-1}\,)
\end{equation}
Altering any block invalidates all subsequent hashes, making tampering
immediately detectable by any party holding a copy of the chain.

S$^3$-Vote uses the blockchain as a \emph{public bulletin board}: all
events are written once and cannot be silently revised.

\subsection{Prior Work on Reputation in Secret Sharing}

Nojoumian and Stinson~\cite{nojoumian2012,nojoumian2018} introduced
\emph{social secret sharing}, where participant reputation influences the
effective threshold. Their key insight — that not all participants should
be treated equally and that past behaviour should inform future requirements
— directly motivates S$^3$-Vote.

However, several critical limitations remain in their model:
(i)~a \emph{trusted dealer} is required to assign and update reputation;
(ii)~reputation updates are social and subjective, opening them to
coalition manipulation;
(iii)~no public proof of cheating is provided;
(iv)~a single threshold gate is used;
(v)~no protection against reputation concentration;
(vi)~no cross-round persistence mechanism is formalised;
(vii)~the work is framed as a secret sharing primitive, not a complete
voting system.
Section~\ref{sec:comparison} provides a detailed per-point comparison.
Cramer, Gennaro, and Schoenmakers~\cite{cramer1997} proposed an early
multi-authority election scheme using threshold ElGamal and zero-knowledge
proofs; S$^3$-Vote builds on this cryptographic foundation while adding
reputation-weighted adaptive thresholds and cross-round accountability.

\subsection{Electronic Voting Systems: State of the Art}
\label{sec:evoting}

Electronic voting has been studied since the early 1980s.
We survey the most influential deployed systems and identify the gaps
that S$^3$-Vote addresses.

\subsubsection*{FOO92}
Fujioka, Okamoto, and Ohta~\cite{foo1992} introduced the first
widely-cited cryptographic e-voting protocol.
FOO92 uses blind signatures to achieve anonymity: each voter obtains a
blind signature from the administrator on their ballot and submits it
anonymously through an untappable channel.
The protocol achieves individual verifiability but relies on a
\emph{single trusted administrator} for signature issuance — a central
point of failure that S$^3$-Vote eliminates through threshold key management.
Furthermore, FOO92 provides no mechanism for handling a compromised or
unresponsive administrator, and offers no accountability trail for
administrator behaviour.

\subsubsection*{Helios}
Helios~\cite{adida2008} is an open-audit web-based voting system that
uses ElGamal encryption and mix-nets or homomorphic tallying.
Ballots are posted on a public bulletin board; any observer can audit
the tally by following the published cryptographic proofs.
Helios achieves end-to-end verifiability in the browser but uses a
\emph{fixed set of trustees with equal trust and a static threshold}.
A compromised or absent trustee stalls the election with no automated
recovery mechanism and no accountability record.
S$^3$-Vote replaces the static equal-trust model with adaptive,
reputation-weighted thresholds and adds automatic failure detection
and penalty assignment.

\subsubsection*{Belenios}
Belenios~\cite{cortier2013} improves upon Helios by introducing a
stronger coercion-resistance model and formal proofs of ballot privacy
and tally correctness; it is deployed in several French academic elections.
Like Helios, Belenios uses a \emph{static trustee model with no reputation
mechanism}, and its threshold is fixed at the start of each election.
There is no formalised mechanism for what happens when trustees
misbehave across multiple elections.

\subsubsection*{Civitas}
Civitas~\cite{clarkson2008} is a coercion-resistant voting system based
on the Juels-Catalano-Jakobsson (JCJ) credential model.
Voters hold credentials that can be used to cast cover votes under
coercion, providing deniability.
Civitas achieves strong coercion resistance but requires a complex
credential distribution ceremony and is computationally expensive
(re-encryption mix-nets and zero-knowledge proofs throughout).
The trustee model is static and offers no cross-round accountability.

\subsubsection*{Blockchain-Based Voting Systems}

A separate line of work places voting logic directly on a public
blockchain. Commercial systems such as Voatz and Follow My Vote deploy
smart contracts on Ethereum so that vote casting and tallying are executed
by the network itself, with no trusted server.
The appeal is maximal decentralisation: consensus is enforced by
thousands of independent nodes.

However, public blockchain voting introduces three problems that
S$^3$-Vote deliberately avoids.
First, \emph{on-chain vote exposure}: Ethereum transactions are public
by default; encrypting ballots on-chain requires additional zero-knowledge
infrastructure that significantly increases complexity and gas cost.
Second, \emph{fixed and public authority set}: Ethereum-based schemes
have no native mechanism for reputation-weighted decryption authorities
whose quorum adapts across rounds.
Third, \emph{throughput and cost}: recording every ballot as a
blockchain transaction is prohibitively expensive and slow for elections
with thousands of voters.

S$^3$-Vote uses the blockchain as a \emph{tamper-evident audit log}
rather than an execution engine.
Vote encryption, share verification, and threshold logic run
off-chain in efficient Python; only the immutable event records
(commitments, share submissions, reputation updates) are written
to the chain.
This design achieves the non-repudiation and transparency benefits
of a public ledger without the throughput, cost, or complexity
penalties of on-chain execution.
In a production deployment, the local chain would be replaced by a
permissioned distributed ledger (e.g., Hyperledger Fabric) shared
among the election authorities — retaining the audit guarantees
while keeping sensitive key material off any public network.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Design Goals}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{G\arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Vote confidentiality} — no party learns any ballot before
    reconstruction; the private key is never held whole.
  \item \textbf{Tally integrity} — votes committed before reconstruction
    cannot be added, removed, or modified.
  \item \textbf{Cheat accountability} — invalid share submissions produce
    publicly verifiable, non-repudiable evidence.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive resilience} — the reconstruction requirement adapts
    to the group's demonstrated trustworthiness.
  \item \textbf{No trusted coordinator} — no single party controls
    reputation, key splitting, or threshold calculation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Threat Model}

\paragraph{Adversary classification.}
We distinguish two orthogonal dimensions of adversarial behaviour.

\textbf{Byzantine vs.\ rational.}
A \emph{Byzantine} (malicious) authority deviates arbitrarily from the
protocol — submitting corrupted shares, lying about state, or simply
going silent — with no regard for their own payoff.
A \emph{rational} authority deviates only when deviation yields higher
expected utility than honest participation.
S$^3$-Vote must withstand both: PVSS provides accountability against
Byzantine behaviour (Section~\ref{sec:security}), while the reputation
penalty mechanism is designed to make honest play the dominant strategy
for rational authorities (Section~\ref{sec:gametheory}).

\textbf{Adaptive vs.\ static corruption.}
A \emph{statically} corrupted authority is compromised before the
protocol begins; the adversary fixes the corrupt set in advance.
An \emph{adaptively} corrupted authority may be compromised at any
point during an election, even after seeing partial protocol state.
We assume \emph{static} corruption in the formal proof
(Theorem~\ref{thm:indcpa}); adaptive corruption resistance is left as
future work and requires a UC-secure threshold scheme.

\paragraph{Formal assumptions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Corrupted authority bound.} At most $t-1$ of the $N$
    authorities are Byzantine, where $t$ is the current Shamir threshold.
    This is the minimum required for information-theoretic share secrecy.
  \item \textbf{Authority capabilities.} A corrupted authority may submit
    an invalid share, refuse to participate, or attempt to collude with
    other corrupted authorities to pool shares.
    They may \emph{not} forge another authority's share (each share is
    bound to its authority index via Lagrange interpolation) or rewrite
    blockchain history.
  \item \textbf{Blockchain integrity.} The chain is append-only and
    hash-consistent. In the prototype this is enforced by the server;
    in production by distributed Byzantine-fault-tolerant
    consensus~\cite{lamport1982}.
  \item \textbf{Cryptographic hardness.} The DDH assumption holds in the
    ElGamal group; SHA-256 is modelled as a random oracle for PVSS
    commitments.
  \item \textbf{Honest voters.} Voters submit well-formed encryptions of
    valid options. Malformed ballots are outside scope; input validation
    is an application-layer concern.
  \item \textbf{Honest key setup.} ElGamal group parameters are generated
    honestly and publicly known. A malicious key-generation ceremony is
    out of scope; production deployments would use a distributed key
    generation (DKG) protocol.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Out of scope.}
Voter coercion and receipt-freeness (addressed in
Section~\ref{sec:security}), Byzantine behaviour at the network layer
(message dropping, reordering), and adaptive authority corruption.

\section{Reputation Model}
\label{sec:reputation}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Behavioural Scoring}

After each election, three dimensions are objectively scored for every
authority $A_i$ based on outcomes recorded on the blockchain:

\subsubsection*{Participation ($P_i$)}
\begin{equation}
  P_i = \begin{cases}
    1.0 & A_i \text{ submitted a valid share} \\
    0.5 & A_i \text{ submitted an invalid share (showed up but cheated)} \\
    0.0 & A_i \text{ was absent or explicitly skipped}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Contribution ($C_i$)}
\begin{equation}
  C_i = \begin{cases}
    \min\!\left(1,\; \dfrac{w_i}{\bar{w}_{\text{needed}}}\right) &
      A_i \text{ submitted a valid share} \\[8pt]
    0.0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{w}_{\text{needed}} = \tau / t$ is the average weight one share
must carry to satisfy the quorum, $\tau$ is the current quorum, and $t$ is
the Shamir threshold.
High-weight authorities naturally score higher here, reflecting that their
participation was disproportionately valuable.

\subsubsection*{Honesty ($H_i$)}
\begin{equation}
  H_i = \begin{cases}
    1.0 & \text{PVSS verification passed} \\
    0.0 & \text{PVSS verification failed (intentional corruption)} \\
    0.5 & \text{absent — neutral, did not actively lie}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
An absent authority receives a neutral honesty score of 0.5 rather than 0
because absence, while costly in contribution, is not equivalent to
actively attempting to sabotage reconstruction.

\subsection{Reputation Update Formula}

\begin{equation}
  \boxed{R_i^{(\ell+1)} = \delta \left[\,
    \lambda R_i^{(\ell)} +
    (1-\lambda)\left(\alpha P_i + \beta C_i + \gamma H_i\right)
  \right]}
  \label{eq:rep}
\end{equation}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{clll}
\toprule
Symbol & Value & Role & Rationale \\
\midrule
$\alpha$ & 0.2 & Participation weight & Necessary but not sufficient \\
$\beta$  & 0.3 & Contribution weight  & Reflects actual value added \\
$\gamma$ & 0.5 & Honesty weight       & Highest: cheating is most damaging \\
$\lambda$ & 0.7 & Memory factor       & Past vs.\ present behaviour \\
$\delta$ & 0.95 & Decay factor        & Small penalty for inactivity \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{remark}
  The memory factor $\lambda = 0.7$ ensures that a single bad election does
  not permanently destroy a long-standing reputation, but that sustained
  misbehaviour accumulates irreversibly. The decay $\delta = 0.95$ provides
  a small passive incentive to remain active across rounds.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Initial Reputation}

New authorities begin at:
\begin{equation}
  R_{\text{initial}} = \frac{2}{3}
\end{equation}
This value is deliberately chosen so that a fully fresh, equal-reputation
group produces a neutral starting quorum of exactly $\lceil N/2 \rceil$
(proven formally in Theorem~\ref{thm:anchor}).

\subsection{Reputation Weights}

Raw scores are normalised into per-authority weights relative to the group:
\begin{equation}
  w_i = \frac{R_i}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{N} R_j} \cdot N
  \label{eq:weight}
\end{equation}
By construction, weights sum to $N$ and average to $1.0$.
An authority with twice the average reputation carries weight $w_i = 2.0$;
one with half the average carries $w_i = 0.5$.

\subsection{Persistence Across Elections}

Reputations are stored in a persistent registry keyed by authority
identity (name in the prototype; public key in a production deployment).
When an authority participates in a new election, their historical score
is loaded automatically. New authorities who have no prior history start
at $R_{\text{initial}} = 2/3$.
This design gives the system \emph{institutional memory}: a long track
record of honest behaviour genuinely lowers future quorum requirements,
while sustained misbehaviour permanently raises them.

\section{Adaptive Threshold}
\label{sec:threshold}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Trust Evenness}

Collective trust is not simply the average reputation — it must also account
for \emph{how evenly} reputation is distributed.
An authority with extremely high reputation relative to peers can accumulate
a disproportionate weight, potentially satisfying the quorum alone if the
threshold were low.
We prevent this via the evenness factor:

\begin{equation}
  E = \max\!\left(0,\; 1 - \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}(R_i - \bar{R})^2}
                                  {N \bar{R}^2}\right)
  \label{eq:evenness}
\end{equation}

$E = 1$ when all reputations are identical (zero variance).
$E \to 0$ as variance grows relative to the mean squared reputation.
The combined trust score is:

\begin{equation}
  \tau_{\text{trust}} = \min\!\left(1,\; \bar{R} \cdot E\right)
  \label{eq:trust}
\end{equation}

\begin{property}[Concentration resistance]
\label{prop:concentration}
  If one authority's weight approaches $N$, evenness $E \to 0$,
  trust $\tau_{\text{trust}} \to 0$, and the quorum rises to
  $\tau_{\text{max}} = N-1$.
  No single authority can ever satisfy the quorum alone.
\end{property}

\subsection{Two-Piece Linear Adaptive Formula}

\begin{definition}[Threshold Parameters]
  For a group of $N$ authorities:
  \begin{align}
    \tau_{\text{neutral}} &= \left\lceil \tfrac{N}{2} \right\rceil
      & &\text{(starting quorum for fresh authorities)} \\
    \tau_{\text{max}}     &= N - 1
      & &\text{(worst-case quorum, no trust)} \\
    \tau_{\text{min}}     &= \max\!\left(2,\, \left\lceil \tfrac{N}{3} \right\rceil\right)
      & &\text{(best-case quorum, full trust)}
  \end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\tau =
    \begin{cases}
      \tau_{\text{max}} -
        \bigl(\tau_{\text{max}} - \tau_{\text{neutral}}\bigr)
        \cdot \tau_{\text{trust}} \cdot \tfrac{3}{2}
        & \text{if } \tau_{\text{trust}} \leq \tfrac{2}{3} \\[10pt]
      \tau_{\text{neutral}} -
        \bigl(\tau_{\text{neutral}} - \tau_{\text{min}}\bigr)
        \cdot \bigl(\tau_{\text{trust}} - \tfrac{2}{3}\bigr) \cdot 3
        & \text{if } \tau_{\text{trust}} > \tfrac{2}{3}
    \end{cases}}
  \label{eq:threshold}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Neutral Anchor]
\label{thm:anchor}
  For $N$ authorities all at $R_i = \tfrac{2}{3}$ (the initial
  reputation), Equation~\eqref{eq:threshold} yields
  $\tau = \lceil N/2 \rceil$ exactly.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  Identical reputations imply zero variance, so $E = 1$.
  Then $\tau_{\text{trust}} = \bar{R} \cdot 1 = \tfrac{2}{3}$.
  Substituting into the first branch of Equation~\eqref{eq:threshold}:
  \begin{align*}
    \tau &= \tau_{\text{max}} -
      \bigl(\tau_{\text{max}} - \tau_{\text{neutral}}\bigr)
      \cdot \tfrac{2}{3} \cdot \tfrac{3}{2} \\
         &= \tau_{\text{max}} -
      \bigl(\tau_{\text{max}} - \tau_{\text{neutral}}\bigr) \\
         &= \tau_{\text{neutral}}
          = \left\lceil \tfrac{N}{2} \right\rceil. \qquad\qquad\square
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\section{Two-Gate Reconstruction Policy}
\label{sec:twogates}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Formal Definition}

Let $\mathcal{S}$ denote the set of authorities who have submitted
valid shares, $t$ the Shamir threshold derived as
$t = \max(2, \lfloor\tau\rfloor)$, and $\tau$ the current adaptive quorum.
Reconstruction is triggered if and only if both gates are simultaneously
satisfied:

\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{|\mathcal{S}| \geq t}_{\text{Gate 1: cryptographic}}
  \quad \textbf{AND} \quad
  \underbrace{\displaystyle\sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} w_i \,\geq\, \tau}_{\text{Gate 2: trust}}
  \label{eq:twogates}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Why Two Gates Are Necessary}

\paragraph{Gate 1 alone — flooding attack.}
Without a weight gate, an adversary controlling many low-reputation
authorities could submit enough shares to satisfy the share count $t$
while their combined weight $\sum w_i \ll \tau$.
The weight gate blocks this.

\paragraph{Gate 2 alone — monopoly attack.}
Without a share count gate, a single authority with very high reputation
could conceivably accumulate weight $w_i \geq \tau$ (though
Property~\ref{prop:concentration} makes this impossible in practice via
the evenness mechanism). The share count gate (minimum $t \geq 2$) provides
an independent, cryptographic line of defence.

\paragraph{Combined protection.}
Together, the two gates ensure that reconstruction requires a coalition
that is both \emph{numerous enough} (cryptographic) and
\emph{trusted enough} (reputational). Neither criterion alone is sufficient.

\subsection{Impossibility Detection}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Real-time impossibility check}
\label{alg:impossible}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Election state: $\mathcal{V}$ (valid), $\mathcal{F}$ (all acted),
         $\mathcal{U} = \mathcal{A} \setminus \mathcal{F}$ (free)
\Ensure \textbf{true} if reconstruction can never be completed
\If{$|\mathcal{V}| + |\mathcal{U}| < t$}
  \Return \textbf{true} \Comment{Insufficient shares possible}
\EndIf
\State $W_{\max} \gets \sum_{i \in \mathcal{V}} w_i + \sum_{i \in \mathcal{U}} w_i$
\If{$W_{\max} < \tau$}
  \Return \textbf{true} \Comment{Insufficient weight possible}
\EndIf
\Return \textbf{false}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Impossibility is checked after every invalid share or skip event.
Upon detection, the system transitions to a \texttt{failed} state
immediately, applies automatic penalties (Equation~\eqref{eq:rep} with
$P=0, C=0, H=0.5$ for skippers; $P=0.5, C=0, H=0$ for cheaters), and
records the failure on the blockchain before any remaining authorities act.

\texttt{reconstruction\_complete} & Round, coalition members, coalition weight, tally \\
\texttt{election\_failed}         & Round, valid shares, cheaters, skippers, failure reason \\
\texttt{reputation\_update}       & Round, updated scores for all authorities, new quorum \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Non-Repudiation Guarantee}

The PVSS commitments $\{C_i\}$ are written in the
\texttt{election\_start} block \emph{before} any share submission.
When authority $A_i$ later submits $\hat{s}_i$ and the check
$g^{H(\hat{s}_i)} \neq C_i$ fails, the \texttt{share\_invalid} event is
chained to the existing commitment block.
$A_i$ cannot subsequently claim:
\begin{itemize}
  \item That the commitment was set incorrectly (it was published before they acted).
  \item That the verification was performed incorrectly (the computation is
    deterministic and anyone can re-run it against the on-chain commitment).
  \item That they never submitted (the \texttt{share\_invalid} event is
    hash-chained and cannot be removed).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Production Deployment Note}

The prototype runs the blockchain as an in-memory hash-chained list on a
single server. In a production deployment, this would be replaced by a
distributed ledger (e.g., Ethereum or Hyperledger Fabric) where no single
party controls the chain and tamper-evidence is enforced by network
consensus. All data models, event formats, and hash-chain properties
remain identical; only the consensus and storage layer changes.
The prototype faithfully demonstrates the full cryptographic and data
flow; the deployment change is an engineering rather than a design concern.

\section{Security Analysis}
\label{sec:security}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Vote Confidentiality}

Individual votes remain confidential until reconstruction because:
(a)~the ElGamal private key is split using Shamir's
information-theoretically secure $(t,n)$-scheme;
(b)~any $t-1$ or fewer shares reveal \emph{zero} information about the key;
(c)~ciphertexts on the blockchain are computationally indistinguishable from
random under the DDH assumption, so even an observer who reads the entire
blockchain during voting learns nothing about ballot contents.

\subsection{Tally Integrity}

Once the \texttt{voting\_closed} block is written and hash-chained, every
\texttt{vote\_cast} block before it is locked into the chain.
Adding a ballot retroactively would require inserting a new block before
\texttt{voting\_closed}, which would change every subsequent block hash —
detectable by any observer.

\subsection{Cheat Detection and Accountability}

PVSS commitments are published in \texttt{election\_start} before any
authority acts. The verification step $g^{H(\hat{s}_i)} \equiv C_i$
is deterministic and publicly replicable. Upon failure, the
\texttt{share\_invalid} block is immediately appended and cannot be
retracted. This provides:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Soundness}: a valid share always passes verification
    (by construction of the commitment).
  \item \textbf{Binding}: a corrupted share cannot pass verification
    (under the discrete logarithm hardness assumption, finding
    $\hat{s}_i \neq s_i$ with $g^{H(\hat{s}_i)} = g^{H(s_i)}$ requires
    a hash collision).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sybil Resistance}

Creating new authority identities does not lower the quorum.
New authorities start at $R_{\text{initial}} = 2/3$ (neutral).
Adding more neutral-reputation authorities increases $N$, which increases
$\tau_{\text{neutral}} = \lceil N/2 \rceil$ proportionally.
A Sybil attacker who floods the authority set with fake identities
actually \emph{raises} the required coalition size.

\subsection{Collusion Resistance}

A colluding coalition $\mathcal{C}$ of low-reputation authorities cannot
satisfy Gate~2 unless $\sum_{i \in \mathcal{C}} w_i \geq \tau$.
Since low-reputation authorities have $w_i < 1$, they must field more
members than an equally sized trusted coalition.
Furthermore, the evenness factor raises $\tau$ when reputation is
heterogeneous, making the required coalition size even larger.

\subsection{Concentration Attack}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Property~\ref{prop:concentration}]
  Suppose authority $A_1$ holds proportion $\theta \in (0,1)$ of total
  reputation, i.e., $R_1 = \theta \sum_j R_j$.
  Then $w_1 = \theta N$.
  \begin{align*}
    \bar{R} &= \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_j R_j, \quad
    (R_1 - \bar{R})^2 = \left(\theta N \bar{R} - \bar{R}\right)^2
                      = \bar{R}^2(\theta N - 1)^2 \\
    E &= \max\!\left(0, 1 - \frac{\bar{R}^2(\theta N-1)^2 + \cdots}
                                  {N\bar{R}^2}\right)
  \end{align*}
  As $\theta \to 1$ (one authority holds all reputation),
  $(R_1-\bar{R})^2 \to \bar{R}^2(N-1)^2$ and the remaining terms also
  contribute positively to the variance numerator, so $E \to 0$,
  $\tau_{\text{trust}} \to 0$, and $\tau \to \tau_{\text{max}} = N-1$.
  Since $w_1 = N\theta < N-1$ for any $\theta < 1$, Gate 2 cannot be
  satisfied by $A_1$ alone. \hfill$\square$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Formal Ballot Privacy Proof}

We formalise ballot privacy as an IND-CPA security game and provide a
concrete reduction to the DDH problem.

\begin{definition}[DDH Assumption~\cite{boneh1998}]
  Let $\mathbb{G} = \langle g \rangle$ be a cyclic group of prime order $q$.
  The \emph{Decisional Diffie-Hellman} (DDH) problem is to distinguish the
  tuple $(g, g^a, g^b, g^{ab})$ from $(g, g^a, g^b, g^c)$ where
  $a, b, c \xleftarrow{R} \mathbb{Z}_q$.
  Formally, for every PPT adversary $\mathcal{B}$:
  \begin{equation}
    \bigl|\Pr[\mathcal{B}(g, g^a, g^b, g^{ab}) = 1]
         - \Pr[\mathcal{B}(g, g^a, g^b, g^c) = 1]\bigr|
    \leq \mathrm{negl}(\lambda)
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[IND-CPA Ballot Privacy Game~\cite{katzlindell2014}]
  Let $\Pi = (\mathsf{KeyGen}, \mathsf{Enc}, \mathsf{Dec})$ be the
  threshold ElGamal scheme of S$^3$-Vote.
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Setup.} Challenger $\mathcal{C}$ runs $\mathsf{KeyGen}$,
      splits private key $x$ via Shamir $(t, N)$, and publishes
      public key $y = g^x$ and PVSS commitments $\{C_i\}$.
    \item \textbf{Challenge.} Adversary $\mathcal{A}$ submits two plaintexts
      $(m_0, m_1)$. $\mathcal{C}$ samples $b \xleftarrow{R} \{0,1\}$ and
      returns $\mathbf{c}^* = \mathsf{Enc}(y, m_b; k^*)$ for fresh random $k^*$.
    \item \textbf{Guess.} $\mathcal{A}$ outputs $b' \in \{0,1\}$.
  \end{enumerate}
  The advantage is
  $\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{IND\text{-}CPA}}_\Pi(\mathcal{A})
  = \bigl|\Pr[b' = b] - \tfrac{1}{2}\bigr|$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Ballot Privacy under DDH]
\label{thm:indcpa}
  If the DDH assumption holds in $\mathbb{G}$, the S$^3$-Vote threshold
  ElGamal scheme achieves IND-CPA ballot privacy.
  Concretely, for every PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$:
  \begin{equation}
    \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{IND\text{-}CPA}}_\Pi(\mathcal{A})
    \;\leq\;
    \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{DDH}}_\mathbb{G}(\mathcal{B})
  \end{equation}
  for a PPT algorithm $\mathcal{B}$ constructed from $\mathcal{A}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  We construct $\mathcal{B}$ that uses $\mathcal{A}$ to solve DDH.

  \textbf{Input to $\mathcal{B}$.}
  $\mathcal{B}$ receives $(g, A, B, X)$ where $A = g^a$, $B = g^b$, and
  $X$ is either $g^{ab}$ (real tuple) or $g^c$ for uniformly random $c$
  (random tuple).

  \textbf{Setup simulation.}
  $\mathcal{B}$ sets public key $y := A = g^a$ (treating the unknown $a$
  as the private key), splits a dummy value via Shamir and publishes
  consistent PVSS commitments. No knowledge of $a$ is required.

  \textbf{Challenge simulation.}
  $\mathcal{A}$ submits $(m_0, m_1)$; $\mathcal{B}$ samples
  $b \xleftarrow{R} \{0,1\}$ and returns:
  \begin{equation}
    \mathbf{c}^* = \bigl(B,\; m_b \cdot X\bigr) = \bigl(g^b,\; m_b \cdot X\bigr)
  \end{equation}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item If $X = g^{ab}$: $\mathbf{c}^* = (g^b,\, m_b \cdot y^b)$, a valid
      ElGamal encryption of $m_b$ under randomness $k = b$.
      $\mathcal{A}$ sees an honestly distributed challenge ciphertext.
    \item If $X = g^c$ for uniformly random $c$: the second component
      $m_b \cdot g^c$ is uniformly random in $\mathbb{G}$, independent of
      $m_b$. The ciphertext carries no information about $b$.
  \end{itemize}

  \textbf{Output.}
  $\mathcal{B}$ outputs whatever $b'$ $\mathcal{A}$ returns.

  \textbf{Advantage analysis.}
  \begin{align*}
    \Pr[\mathcal{B}\text{ wins} \mid X = g^{ab}]
      &= \Pr[b' = b] = \tfrac{1}{2} + \epsilon \\
    \Pr[\mathcal{B}\text{ wins} \mid X = g^c]
      &= \Pr[b' = b] = \tfrac{1}{2}
  \end{align*}
  Therefore
  $\mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{DDH}}(\mathcal{B}) = \epsilon
   = \mathsf{Adv}^{\mathsf{IND\text{-}CPA}}(\mathcal{A})$.
  Since DDH is assumed hard, $\epsilon \leq \mathrm{negl}(\lambda)$.
  \hfill$\square$

  \textbf{Threshold extension.}
  The private key $x$ is split via Shamir $(t,N)$; any $t-1$ or fewer
  shares reveal \emph{zero} information about $x$ by information-theoretic
  secrecy~\cite{shamir1979}. An adversary colluding with up to $t-1$
  authorities still learns nothing about $x$, so the IND-CPA argument above
  applies without modification.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[PVSS Binding under DL and ROM]
  Commitments $C_i = g^{H(s_i)} \bmod p$ are binding under two standard
  assumptions. Under \emph{discrete logarithm} (DL) hardness, finding
  $\hat{s}_i \neq s_i$ with $g^{H(\hat{s}_i)} = g^{H(s_i)}$ requires
  first producing a hash collision $H(\hat{s}_i) = H(s_i)$.
  In the \emph{random oracle model} (ROM), $H$ is modelled as a uniformly
  random function; a collision occurs with probability at most $1/q$
  ($\approx 2^{-256}$ for standard parameters).
  Consequently, any invalid share submitted by an authority is detected
  with overwhelming probability, and the \texttt{share\_invalid} blockchain
  record constitutes a publicly verifiable, non-repudiable proof of cheating.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Receipt-Freeness and Coercion Resistance}

\paragraph{The coercion problem.}
In the ElGamal scheme as deployed in S$^3$-Vote, a voter who encrypts
ballot $m$ with randomness $k$ can later prove their vote to a coercer by
revealing $k$: the coercer verifies $C_1 = g^k$ and $C_2 = m \cdot y^k$.
This enables vote-buying and voter coercion.
A \emph{receipt-free} protocol prevents any voter from constructing such
a proof, even if they actively cooperate with the coercer.

\paragraph{Mix-net integration.}
A \emph{mix-net}~\cite{chaum1988} routes ciphertexts through a sequence of
mix servers $M_1, \ldots, M_\ell$.
Each server re-randomises every ciphertext using a fresh ephemeral factor
and randomly permutes the list:
\begin{equation}
  (C_1, C_2) \;\xrightarrow{M_j}\; (C_1 \cdot g^{k'}, C_2 \cdot y^{k'})
  \quad \text{for fresh } k' \xleftarrow{R} \mathbb{Z}_q
\end{equation}
Re-randomisation is valid for ElGamal: the new ciphertext still decrypts
to $m$.
After all servers act, the output list encrypts the same multiset of votes
but with fresh, unknown randomness, and no voter can link their original
ciphertext to an output position — provided at least one mix server is honest.

Integrating a mix-net into S$^3$-Vote requires no changes to the reputation,
threshold, or blockchain subsystems.
The modification is purely in the election lifecycle: a \emph{mix phase}
is inserted between \texttt{voting\_closed} and the reconstruction phase.
The decryption authorities operate on the mixed output, not the raw ballots.

\paragraph{Homomorphic tallying.}
ElGamal is multiplicatively homomorphic:
\begin{equation}
  \mathsf{Enc}(m_1) \cdot \mathsf{Enc}(m_2) = \mathsf{Enc}(m_1 \cdot m_2)
\end{equation}
Encoding a vote for option $v$ as $m = g^v$, the product of all $M$
encrypted ballots satisfies:
\begin{equation}
  \prod_{j=1}^{M} (C_{1,j}, C_{2,j})
  = \Bigl(g^{\sum k_j},\; g^{\sum v_j} \cdot y^{\sum k_j}\Bigr)
  = \mathsf{Enc}\!\left(g^{\sum v_j}\right)
\end{equation}
The authorities decrypt \emph{only this aggregate ciphertext}, recovering
$g^{\sum v_j}$, from which $\sum v_j$ is extracted by discrete logarithm
(feasible for small vote counts by baby-step giant-step).
Individual ballots are never decrypted; a voter therefore cannot prove
their contribution to the aggregate tally.

Integrating homomorphic tallying into S$^3$-Vote requires two changes:
(a)~the server accumulates the running product of ciphertexts as votes
arrive; (b)~reconstruction operates on the single aggregate ciphertext.
All other components — reputation, threshold, PVSS, blockchain — are
unaffected.

\paragraph{Comparison of approaches.}
Homomorphic tallying is the simpler integration path: no additional servers
or shuffle proofs are required, and the existing single-round decryption
structure is preserved.
Mix-nets provide stronger compositional guarantees and handle ranked or
multi-option elections more naturally.
Both approaches are direct extensions of the current prototype and do not
require redesigning any other component of S$^3$-Vote.

\subsection{End-to-End Verifiability}
\label{sec:e2ev}

End-to-end verifiability (E2E-V) requires that any party can verify:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Their ballot was recorded as submitted (\emph{cast-as-intended});
  \item All recorded ballots were included in the tally (\emph{recorded-as-cast});
  \item The tally was computed correctly (\emph{tallied-as-recorded}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Individual verifiability.}
After submitting ballot ciphertext $(C_1, C_2)$, a voter can verify that
the exact ciphertext appears in the \texttt{vote\_cast} blocks of the
blockchain. Since the chain is publicly readable and hash-chained, this
requires no trust in the server.
In a production deployment, each voter receives a cryptographic receipt
$r = H(\text{block\_hash} \| C_1 \| C_2)$,
verifiable against the published chain at any time.

\paragraph{Universal verifiability.}
After the election closes, anyone can independently re-derive the tally:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Collect all \texttt{vote\_cast} ciphertexts between
    \texttt{election\_start} and \texttt{voting\_closed} blocks.
  \item Verify each submitted share against the on-chain PVSS commitment:
    $g^{H(\hat{s}_i)} \equiv C_i \pmod{p}$.
  \item Run Lagrange interpolation on the valid shares to recover $x$.
  \item Decrypt all ciphertexts and re-count.
\end{enumerate}
All inputs — ciphertexts, commitments, shares — are immutably on-chain.
The computation is deterministic and secret-free: any auditor reaches
the same result.

\paragraph{Tally integrity under hash-chain.}
For the tally to be falsified an adversary must either insert a fake
\texttt{vote\_cast} block (requiring recomputation of all subsequent
hashes, detectable by any observer holding a chain snapshot) or delete
a block (causing an immediate hash mismatch at the next block).
Neither is possible without controlling all nodes in a distributed
deployment.

\section{Game-Theoretic Incentive Analysis}
\label{sec:gametheory}
% =============================================================================

A protocol is only as secure as the incentives it creates.
We model S$^3$-Vote as a repeated game and show that honest behaviour
is the dominant strategy for rational authorities.

\subsection{Model}

Let $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, \ldots, A_N\}$ be the authority set.
In each round $\ell$, every authority chooses
$\sigma_i^\ell \in \{\textit{honest},\, \textit{cheat},\, \textit{skip}\}$.

Let $B > 0$ be the shared benefit of successful key reconstruction,
$p_c$ the reputation penalty for cheating, and $p_s$ the penalty for
skipping. The one-round payoff is:

\begin{equation}
  u_i(\sigma_i, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{-i}) =
  \begin{cases}
    B / |\mathcal{S}| - c_h
      & \sigma_i = \textit{honest}, \text{ reconstruction succeeds} \\
    0
      & \sigma_i = \textit{honest}, \text{ reconstruction fails (not } A_i\text{'s fault)} \\
    -p_c
      & \sigma_i = \textit{cheat} \\
    -p_s
      & \sigma_i = \textit{skip}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $c_h$ is the small cost of honest participation and
$\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{-i}$ denotes all other authorities' strategies.

Crucially, payoffs are not one-shot: reputation persists across rounds.
Higher reputation produces lower future quorums, making reconstruction
easier and $B/|\mathcal{S}|$ achievable with fewer abortive rounds.

\subsection{Cheating is a Dominated Strategy}

\begin{theorem}[Honesty Dominance]
\label{thm:dominance}
  Under the S$^3$-Vote reputation model, for a sufficiently long
  horizon $T$, the strategy $\sigma_i = \textit{honest}$ strictly
  dominates $\sigma_i = \textit{cheat}$ for every authority.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
  Suppose $A_i$ cheats in round $\ell$.

  \textbf{(1) Immediate payoff: zero benefit.}
  The corrupted share is immediately rejected by PVSS verification
  (Remark on binding, Section~\ref{sec:security}).
  Cheating does not advance reconstruction; there is no round in which
  cheating produces a higher immediate payoff than honest participation.

  \textbf{(2) Reputation loss.}
  The cheat sets $P_i = 0.5$, $C_i = 0$, $H_i = 0$.
  The behavioural score is $\alpha \cdot 0.5 = 0.1$ (for $\alpha = 0.2$),
  compared to $1.0$ for honest participation.
  Substituting into Equation~\eqref{eq:rep}:
  \begin{align*}
    R_i^{(\ell+1)\text{ cheat}}  &= 0.95[0.7 R_i^{(\ell)} + 0.3 \cdot 0.1]
      \approx 0.665 R_i^{(\ell)} + 0.029 \\
    R_i^{(\ell+1)\text{ honest}} &= 0.95[0.7 R_i^{(\ell)} + 0.3 \cdot 1.0]
      = 0.665 R_i^{(\ell)} + 0.285
  \end{align*}
  The reputation gap is $0.285 - 0.029 = 0.256$ per round of cheating.

  \textbf{(3) Compounding future cost.}
  The lower reputation reduces $w_i$ in all subsequent rounds.
  Since the quorum $\tau$ depends on collective trust
  (Equation~\eqref{eq:threshold}), the cheater's degraded weight makes
  future reconstructions harder for the whole group — including themselves —
  reducing the probability of earning $B/|\mathcal{S}|$ in future rounds.

  \textbf{(4) Long-run dominance.}
  The cumulative payoff loss from a single cheat grows as $O(T)$
  (recovery takes $\Theta(\log(1/(1-\lambda)))$ rounds) while the
  one-round gain from cheating is exactly 0.
  For any $T > T_0 = O(1/\epsilon)$ where $\epsilon$ is the marginal
  detection probability complement, honesty strictly dominates.
  \hfill$\square$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Coalition Instability}

\begin{property}[Coalition Prisoner's Dilemma]
\label{prop:coalition}
  A coalition $\mathcal{C}$ planning to collectively delay or corrupt
  reconstruction faces a prisoner's dilemma: each member individually
  prefers to defect to honesty while the others cheat.
\end{property}

If $\sum_{i \in \mathcal{C}} w_i < \tau$ (Gate 2 unsatisfied), the coalition
gains nothing — reconstruction fails regardless, and all members absorb
reputation losses.
If the coalition holds sufficient weight but deliberately delays, the
impossibility detector (Algorithm~\ref{alg:impossible}) may trigger,
applying skip penalties to all remaining free authorities.
If even one coalition member defects and submits a valid share, that
member earns an honest reputation update while the others do not.
Since defection strictly dominates cooperation, rational coalitions
unravel at the first opportunity.

\subsection{Sybil Rationality Analysis}

An adversary flooding the authority set with $k$ fake identities does
not lower the quorum.
Each fake identity starts at $R_{\text{initial}} = 2/3$ with weight 1.0,
so $k$ fake authorities add weight $k$ to the group.
However, the neutral quorum increases from $\lceil N/2 \rceil$ to
$\lceil (N+k)/2 \rceil$, rising by $\lceil k/2 \rceil$.
The net weight surplus gained by the Sybil adversary is only
$k - \lceil k/2 \rceil = \lfloor k/2 \rfloor$,
while the cost of operating $k$ live authority clients grows linearly in $k$.
For any fixed target excess weight, the number of required fake authorities
grows quadratically, making large-scale Sybil attacks economically irrational.

\section{Parameter Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:sensitivity}
% =============================================================================

The reputation formula (Equation~\eqref{eq:rep}) contains five parameters.
We systematically analyse how varying each parameter affects system behaviour,
providing an empirical basis for the default values.

\subsection{Effect of Memory Factor \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda}}

The memory factor $\lambda \in (0,1)$ controls the balance between
historical reputation and current-round performance.

The long-run equilibrium reputation for an always-honest authority is
obtained by solving $R^* = \delta[\lambda R^* + (1-\lambda)]$, giving:
\begin{equation}
  R^*_{\lambda} = \frac{\delta(1-\lambda)}{1 - \delta\lambda}
  \label{eq:equil}
\end{equation}

Substituting $\delta = 0.95$: for $\lambda = 0.7$, $R^* \approx 0.851$;
for $\lambda = 0.3$, $R^* \approx 0.929$;
for $\lambda = 0.9$, $R^* \approx 0.655$ — \emph{below} the initial
$R_{\text{initial}} = 2/3$.

This last result reveals an important constraint: when $\lambda$ is very
high, the decay factor $\delta < 1$ dominates, and even perfect performance
cannot overcome the passive erosion.
The default $\lambda = 0.7$ sits in the regime where honest authorities
converge to $R^* \approx 0.85$, well above the neutral starting point.

\subsection{Effect of Decay Factor \texorpdfstring{$\delta$}{delta}}

The decay factor $\delta \in (0,1)$ multiplies every reputation update,
providing a passive incentive for authorities to remain active.

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\delta = 1.0$: no decay. An authority that participated once and
    went dormant retains their reputation indefinitely, becoming a
    \emph{ghost authority} that occupies weight without contributing shares.
  \item $\delta = 0.95$ (default): a dormant authority (skipping every round)
    converges to zero over approximately
    $\log(0.01)/\log(0.95) \approx 90$ rounds.
  \item $\delta < 0.85$: rapid erosion; honest authorities' reputations
    also degrade noticeably, destabilising the long-run equilibrium and
    potentially raising the quorum even for honest groups.
\end{itemize}

The default $\delta = 0.95$ removes ghost authorities on a scale of
tens of rounds while allowing honest authorities to maintain reputations
well above the initial $2/3$ (as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:reptraj}).

The sensitivity analysis confirms the design rationale — $\gamma$ large

$\lambda$ moderate for memory-reactivity balance, $\delta$ near-1 for
gentle passive pressure — and bounds the consequences of miscalibration.

\section{Comparison with Prior Work}
\label{sec:comparison}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Against Shamir's Threshold Scheme}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{4.5cm}p{4.5cm}p{4.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Property} & \textbf{Shamir (1979)} & \textbf{S$^3$-Vote} \\
\midrule
Share equality & All shares equal & Reputation-weighted \\
Cheat detection & None & PVSS — immediate, on-chain \\
Threshold & Fixed & Adaptive (trust-driven) \\
Authority accountability & None & Persistent, cross-round \\
Failed round handling & Not addressed & Detect + penalise + recover \\
Voting application & Not addressed & Complete protocol \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Shamir's scheme guarantees information-theoretic secrecy and correct
reconstruction when at least $t$ honest shares are combined.
It makes no provision for identifying which authorities submitted
corrupted shares, nor for adjusting future requirements based on
observed behaviour. S$^3$-Vote builds on its mathematical foundation
while addressing all these gaps.

\subsection{Against Nojoumian's Social Secret Sharing}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{4cm}p{4.5cm}p{4.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Property} & \textbf{Nojoumian} & \textbf{S$^3$-Vote} \\
\midrule
Trusted dealer & Required & None \\
Reputation source & Social / peer evaluation & On-chain, deterministic \\
Cheat detection & Implicit (failure only) & PVSS — public proof \\
Reconstruction gate & Single (weighted) & Dual (shares AND weight) \\
Concentration protection & None & Evenness factor \\
Neutral quorum anchor & Ad hoc & $\lceil N/2 \rceil$ (proven) \\
Failed election recovery & Not addressed & Automatic \\
Audit trail & Database & Blockchain \\
Complete voting system & No & Yes \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Trusted dealer elimination.}
Nojoumian requires a coordinator to assign and update reputations.
S$^3$-Vote is fully self-governing: scores are computed deterministically
from Equation~\eqref{eq:rep} applied to PVSS-verified outcomes.

\paragraph{Objective reputation.}
Nojoumian's social evaluation is gameable: colluding authorities can
inflate each other's scores. S$^3$-Vote scores are derived from
cryptographically verifiable events; there is no mechanism to inflate
a reputation other than genuinely behaving well.

\paragraph{Two-gate policy.}
A single adjusted threshold leaves open the flooding attack (many
low-weight shares meet the count) and the monopoly attack (one very
high-weight authority meets the weight alone).
Equation~\eqref{eq:twogates} closes both simultaneously.

\paragraph{Provable neutral anchor.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:anchor} proves that the quorum initialises at
$\lceil N/2 \rceil$ for any group size, providing a principled and
consistent baseline. Nojoumian's scheme has no equivalent guarantee.

\paragraph{Graceful failure.}
When reconstruction becomes mathematically impossible mid-round, the
system detects this via Algorithm~\ref{alg:impossible}, transitions
to a failed state, applies penalties to responsible parties, and
unlocks the next round — a lifecycle that Nojoumian's model does
not address.

\subsection{Key Algorithms in Code}

The adaptive threshold (Equation~\eqref{eq:threshold}) is implemented as:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Adaptive threshold computation in \texttt{threshold.py}}]
def adaptive_threshold(self, reputations):
    n         = len(reputations)
    avg       = float(np.mean(reputations))
    E         = self.trust_evenness(reputations)
    trust     = min(1.0, avg * E)

    t_neutral = math.ceil(n / 2)
    t_max     = n - 1
    t_min     = max(2, math.ceil(n / 3))

    if trust <= 2 / 3:
        threshold = t_max - (t_max - t_neutral) * trust * 1.5
    else:
        threshold = t_neutral - (t_neutral - t_min) * (trust - 2/3) * 3

    return round(float(max(t_min, min(t_max, threshold))), 3)
\end{lstlisting}

The two-gate check is implemented as:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Two-gate reconstruction trigger in \texttt{app.py}}]
if (len(election['submitted_shares']) >= election['shamir_t']
        and coalition_weight >= election['threshold']):
    _reconstruct()
\end{lstlisting}

Impossibility detection runs after every cheat or skip:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Impossibility detection (Algorithm~\ref{alg:impossible})}]
def _check_impossible():
    acted      = set(submitted) | set(cheaters) | set(skipped)
    still_free = [a for a in authority_names if a not in acted]
    max_shares = len(submitted) + len(still_free)
    if max_shares < shamir_t:
        return True
    max_weight = sum(weights[a] for a in submitted) + \
                 sum(weights[a] for a in still_free)
    if max_weight < threshold:
        return True
    return False
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Example Walkthrough}
\label{sec:walkthrough}
% =============================================================================

We trace a complete two-round scenario with $N = 7$ authorities, all
starting fresh.

\subsection{Round 1 — Initial State}

All 7 authorities at $R_i = \tfrac{2}{3}$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:anchor},
$\tau = 4$, $t = \lfloor 4 \rfloor = 4$.
Equal weights: $w_i = 1.0$ for all $i$.

Suppose 5 submit valid shares, 1 cheats, and 1 skips.
The coalition of 5 achieves:
\begin{align*}
  |\mathcal{S}| &= 5 \geq t = 4 \quad \checkmark \\
  \textstyle\sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} w_i &= 5.0 \geq \tau = 4 \quad \checkmark
\end{align*}
Reconstruction succeeds after the 5th valid submission.

\subsection{Round 2 — Reputations Loaded from Registry}

Updated reputations: 5 honest at 0.728, cheater at 0.472, skipper at 0.515.

\begin{align*}
  \bar{R} &= \tfrac{5(0.728)+0.472+0.515}{7} = \tfrac{4.627}{7} \approx 0.661 \\
  \text{Variance} &\approx 0.0043 \quad \Rightarrow \quad
  E \approx 1 - \tfrac{0.0043}{7 \times 0.661^2} \approx 0.986 \\
  \tau_{\text{trust}} &= \min(1,\; 0.661 \times 0.986) \approx 0.652 < \tfrac{2}{3}\\
  \tau &= 6 - (6-4)(0.652)(1.5) \approx 4.04

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
% =============================================================================

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item[Single-node blockchain.]
    The prototype stores the chain in memory on one server.
    A production deployment requires a distributed ledger with Byzantine
    fault-tolerant consensus.

  \item[Simulated authority clients.]
    All authority actions are performed by a single administrator in the
    dashboard. In a real deployment, each authority would operate an
    independent client holding their private key locally, never sharing it
    with the server.

  \item[Cryptographic identity.]
    Authority names are currently plain strings. In production, each
    authority has a public/private key pair; their identity is their public
    key, and every share submission is signed with their private key and
    verified on-chain. This prevents impersonation and false reputation
    attribution.

  \item[Parameter calibration.]
    The reputation weights ($\alpha{=}0.2$, $\beta{=}0.3$, $\gamma{=}0.5$)
    and smoothing parameters ($\lambda{=}0.7$, $\delta{=}0.95$) were set by
    design rationale. Empirical calibration over realistic election scenarios
    and adversarial benchmarks would strengthen the choices.

\end{description}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

S$^3$-Vote demonstrates that threshold-cryptographic voting can be made
fully self-governing: no trusted coordinator, no static parameters, no
silent failures, and no impunity for misbehaviour.

The core insight is that the reconstruction threshold should be a
\emph{function of demonstrated trustworthiness}, not a fixed design
parameter. By measuring trustworthiness objectively through
PVSS-verified behavioural events, converting it to weights, and
mapping it to a quorum through a provably anchored formula, the system
creates a dynamic equilibrium: honest groups earn efficiency (lower
quorum); dishonest or heterogeneous groups face proportionally higher
requirements.

The two-gate reconstruction policy ensures that neither numerical
flooding nor reputational monopoly can shortcut the security requirement.
The concentration-resistance property formally closes the single
dominant-authority attack vector. And the blockchain audit trail
ensures that every act of honesty or betrayal is permanently,
publicly recorded — creating real accountability where classical
threshold schemes offer none.

Compared to Shamir's foundational work, S$^3$-Vote adds
full accountability, adaptive security, and graceful failure recovery.
Compared to Nojoumian's social secret sharing, it eliminates the
trusted dealer, replaces subjective social evaluation with objective
on-chain evidence, introduces a principled dual-gate reconstruction
policy, and delivers a complete, deployable voting system with
end-to-end verifiability.

The prototype confirms that all components — ElGamal encryption,
Shamir sharing, PVSS verification, reputation scoring, adaptive threshold,
and blockchain logging — integrate correctly and perform the intended
security functions.
A formal IND-CPA ballot privacy proof establishes that ciphertext
indistinguishability reduces to the DDH problem, and a game-theoretic
analysis proves that honest participation is the dominant strategy for
rational authorities over any sufficiently long horizon.
A parameter sensitivity study confirms that the system remains
qualitatively correct across a wide range of weight configurations,
bounding the consequences of miscalibration.

The path to a production system requires distributing the blockchain
across independent nodes, equipping each authority with their own signing
key, and extending the adversary model to adaptive corruption via a
UC-secure threshold scheme — well-understood directions that do not
affect the correctness of the current design.
